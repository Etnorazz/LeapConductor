Leap Overview

Notice: This developer release of Leap Motion, Inc. software is confidential and intended for very limited distribution. Parties using this software must accept the SDK Agreement prior to obtaining this software and related documentation. This software and documentation are subject to copyright.

The Leap detects and tracks hands, fingers and finger-like tools. The device operates in an intimate proximity with high precision and tracking frame rate.

The Leap software analyzes the objects observed in the device field of view. It recognizes hands, fingers, and tools, updating your application at regular intervals. The Leap field of view is an inverted pyramid centered on the device. The effective range of the Leap extends from approximately 25 to 600 millimeters above the device (1 inch to 2 feet).

Coordinate system

The Leap employs a right-handed Cartesian coordinate system. Values reported are in units of real-world millimeters. The origin is centered at the center of the Leap device. The x- and z-axes lie in the horizontal plane, with the x-axis running parallel to the long edge of the device. The y-axis is vertical, with positive values increasing upwards (in contrast to the downward orientation of most computer graphics coordinate systems). The z-axis has positive values increasing away from the computer screen.

The Leap right-handed coordinate system.

Motion tracking data

As the Leap tracks hands, fingers, and tools in its field of view, it provides updates as a set, or frame, of data. Each frame contains a list of the hands detected in that frame. Each hand contains a list of its detected fingers and attached tools. When the Leap detects a hand, finger, or tool, it assigns a unique ID designator to the detected entity. The ID remains the same as long as that entity remains visible within the device's field of view. If tracking is lost and regained, the Leap may assign a new ID (the software may not know that the hand or finger is the same as the one visible earlier).

Note: We plan to enhance the motion tracking data provided to your application before the Leap is released to consumers. In future releases of the Leap SDK, we plan to introduce a skeletal hand model to provide more detailed tracking data and continuity through time. We also plan to introduce gesture recognition for a small set of commonly used gestures.

Hand model

The hand model provides information about the position, shape, orientation, and movement of a detected hand as well as a list of the fingers and tools attached to the hand.

The Leap API provides as much information about a hand as possible. However, the Leap may not be able to determine all hand attributes in every frame. For example, when a hand is clenched into a fist, its fingers are not visible to the Leap so the finger list will be empty. Similarly, if a finger is visible but its hand is outside the edge of the Leap's field of view, the hand properties such as palm and ball will not be available. Your code should handle the cases where an attribute in the model is not available.

The Leap does not determine whether a hand is a left or right hand. More than two hands can appear in the hand list for a frame if more than one person's hands or other hand-like objects are in view. However, we recommend keeping at most two hands in the Leap device's field of view for optimal motion tracking quality.

Palm vectors

The palm ray provides the basic position and orientation of the hand. The position is the center of the palm measured in millimeters from the origin of the Leap coordinate system. The direction vector points from the palm position toward the wrist. The palm normal vector points downward out of the palm, perpendicular to the plane tangent by the palm.

The palm ray provides the palm position and palm-to-wrist direction. The palm normal points perpendicularly out of the hand.

In addition to the hand's wrist and normal vectors, the hand model defines a velocity vector providing the speed and movement direction of the palm in millimeters per second.

Hand ball

The hand ball object provides the position and radius of a sphere fit to the curvature of the hand, as if it were holding a ball.

The size of the sphere decreases as the fingers are curled.

Finger and Tool model

The finger model provides the physical characteristics of a finger or finger-like tool. The reported properties include an ID, length, width, tip position, pointing direction, and velocity vectors for each finger or finger-like tool. As with the hand, position is measured in millimeters from the Leap origin. The position given is for the tip of the finger or tool. The velocity represents the rate of change in tip position, in millimeters per second.

Finger tip rays provide the positions of the finger tips and the directions in which the fingers are pointing.

The Leap classifies a detected member as either a finger or a tool. Use the Finger::isTool() function to determine which one a Finger object represents.

A tool is longer, thinner, and straighter than a finger.
